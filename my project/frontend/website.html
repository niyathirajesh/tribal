Kovilmala (Kozhimala) ‚Äì The Tribal Kingdom

Kovilmala, also called Kozhimala , is a historic tribal village near Kattappana in Idukki, Kerala. It is the only surviving tribal kingdom in South India and the center of the Mannan community.

The kingdom has four regions: Thekkottu Kattu Rajyam, Nadukkuda Kattu Rajyam, Athal Orupuram, and Chenkanattu Mala. Their tutelary deity is Goddess Madhura Minakshi from Madura Temple, Tamil Nadu.

The king, known as Raja Mannan, is elected by the people and guided by nine ministers called Kanis. They oversee 42 settlements, settle disputes, and preserve traditions.

The Kozhimala (Kovilmala) Mannan community of Idukki district represents a rare and significant example of an indigenous society that continues to practice a democratic monarchy while adapting to modern socio-economic changes.

Rituals & Ethnobotany

Festivals and Rituals

Kalavoottu is the annual ceremonial feast of the Mannan community, symbolizing gratitude for blessings and prayers for prosperity. Historically observed over seven days, it included Mannan Koothu, a traditional performance narrating the story of Kannaki and Kovalan. Today, the festival is celebrated over a single day but retains its spirit of devotion, cultural pride, and community unity. Kalavoottu is preceded by Payel Iruthal, a seven-day mourning ritual for departed souls, ensuring spiritual preparation and collective harmony before festivities.

TMannankoothu is the community‚Äôs ritualistic art form, based entirely on the epic Silappathikaram. Traditionally performed by men‚Äîeven in female roles‚Äîit combines storytelling, music, and dance. Mannankoothu accompanies major festivals like Kalavoottu and Pongal, as well as important life events such as marriages and harvests. While once performed over seven days, modern adaptations often condense it to a single night while striving to preserve its cultural and spiritual depth.

Meenoottu is an annual ritual held three to four days after Kalavoottu to appease deities and seek protection. The King, along with select community members, offers rice to river fishes in a sacred ceremony, interpreting their movements as divine signs. The ritual includes prayers, purification, and a communal feast, reinforcing devotion and ancestral connection. Despite environmental changes and modern challenges, the Mannan people continue Meenoottu with dedication.

Pongal, another major festival, is celebrated in temples and sacred groves a month after Kalavoottu. The community gathers for rituals, traditional Koothu performances, and offerings to 42 clan deities. The festival emphasizes unity, blessings, and communal joy, drawing devotees from within and outside the community.

Cultural Heritage

The Mannan community has maintained its traditions for generations, with a strong focus on ritual, art, and respect for nature. Their ceremonies, songs, and performances not only honor ancestors and deities but also reinforce identity and social cohesion. Today, efforts are being made to adapt cultural practices for tourism and education while safeguarding the authenticity of rituals like Mannankoothu and Meenoottu.

The Mannan are an indigenous tribal community mainly residing in Idukki district, Kerala, with smaller populations in Ernakulam and Thrissur. Renowned for their unique cultural identity and traditional kingship, they are one of the few tribes in India with a king.

üèõÔ∏è Historical Origins

The history of the Mannan community traces back to the Chola dynasty in Tamil Nadu. According to ancestral narratives, the Mannans originally lived in Madurai. Their migration to the forests of Kerala is preserved through oral traditions passed down across generations.

During the Chola‚ÄìPandya wars in Madurai, members of the Mannan community entered forest regions to collect materials and accessories for warfare ‚öîÔ∏è. Another account reveals that the Mannans were once considered slaves under Chola rule, performing heavy labor such as pulling the chariot of the Madurai Meenakshi Temple. This task required rattan stems üåø, which led many Mannans into the forests of Kerala.

While collecting rattan, the Mannans discovered the beauty, safety, and comfort of forest life, which gradually persuaded them to remain instead of returning to their homeland.

üå≥ Migration and Settlement

A fascinating oral tradition recounts their permanent settlement in Kerala. When the Mannans reached the Puliyanmala forest, they found a tortoise egg üê¢, which they cooked and ate. This symbolic event marked their decision to stay permanently, embracing the forest as their true home.

üëë Kingship and Social Structure

Today, the Mannan community is unique in India for maintaining a traditional kingship. They are one of only two tribal communities in India with a king, and the only one in Kerala. The current ruler, Rama Rajamannan, is the 14th king of the Mannan dynasty.

In addition, the community follows an internal caste system, comprising 37 sub-castes, which continues to guide social organization and cultural practices.

üåü Present Day

Approximately 50,000 Mannans live in Kerala today. Despite modernization, they have successfully preserved their customs, rituals, and traditional governance, making them a culturally significant and unique community in India.
